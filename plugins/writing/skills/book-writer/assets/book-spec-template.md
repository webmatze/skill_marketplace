**Pastiche anchor** (optional but the single strongest lever for a human voice):

> Paste 1–3 paragraphs of *real* prose whose voice this book should resemble — a passage from one of the comp titles, another author the user loves, or (best of all) something the user has written themselves. This is not to copy; it's a concrete style model to measure drafts against. "Does this read like the anchor?" is a sharper question than "does this read well?" If the user can supply their own writing, prefer that — the goal is *their* voice, not a generic good one.

**Anti-patterns** (what this voice never does — as defining as what it does):

> List the moves this narrator avoids. Examples: "never uses semicolons", "no rhetorical questions to the reader", "never names an emotion outright", "no five-dollar Latinate words", "never opens a chapter with weather", "doesn't explain its own jokes". A voice is shaped as much by its refusals as by its habits.

**Idiosyncrasies & obsessions** (what makes the voice a *person*, not a competent average):

> The deliberate quirks that competent-but-anonymous prose lacks. A recurring motif or running joke; a domain the narrator's metaphors all come from (a cook thinks in kitchen images, an ex-soldier in tactical ones); a subject the narrator is unfair or biased about; an opinion the book holds and doesn't balance. Give the voice at least one thing it cares about too much.

## Voice ledger (accumulating — fill during drafting, not up front)

> Leave this empty at the start. The continuity ledger (in `outline.md`) keeps the *facts* consistent across chapters; this ledger keeps the *voice* consistent. Without it, voice drifts over 25 chapters and the back half reads differently — and more generically — than the front. Add a line whenever you make a concrete style decision so the next chapter inherits it instead of re-deciding.
>
> Record the small, specific, mechanical choices a human writer is consistent about by instinct:
>
> - Spelling/usage picks: "okay" not "in Ordnung"; "ß" vs "ss" convention; numbers as words or digits
> - Punctuation habits: uses comma splices in interiority; em-dashes rare; no semicolons
> - Contractions and register: how spoken vs. narrated text differs
> - Recurring motifs/metaphor domain already established (so they stay consistent, not random)
> - Things tried and rejected: "we decided the narrator does NOT address the reader directly"
>
> Load this before drafting each chapter and update it after. Example entries:
>
> - [ch.1] Narrator never names emotions; renders via the hands. Keep.
> - [ch.3] Dialogue uses regional particles (na, gell); narration stays standard.
> - [ch.5] Rejected: dream sequences. Don't reintroduce.
